Part 1: Group Charter

The Group Charter is an important part of the group’s success. This will help everyone set and manage expectations, workload, and to develop a plan for communication and decision-making. The Charter will also document the agreed-upon roles and responsibilities for each member (as presented in the Group Roles Activity). This is a contract between each group member, and must be agreed upon and approved by each member.

Your Group Charter should include the following items:

1. Expected technical roles of each team member

Every group must account for the 5 functional roles in some way.

  1. Assign the baseline roles (Code Monkey, Project Manager, Technical Communictions Manager) of each member. Every team member should have 1 of these roles. You can define new ones, but use the given roles as a guide.
  2. Decide how you will dole out the additional Auxiliary Roles (QA/QC, Resource Manager). For example, you can rotate them for each deliverable, or pick one person who will manage it for the whole project.
WarningFAQs
  • Can we switch roles during the project?
  • How many code monkeys do we need?

2. Scheduling

  1. Indicate a weekly outside-class meeting time slot, during which everyone agrees they could be free to meet. You don’t need to always meet at this time, but make sure that there exists at least one weekly time in our space-time continuum that works for everyone. This is required.
  2. Discuss whether anyone has any foreseeable schedule disruptions that might affect the team (conference travel, on-site job interviews, part/full time work, midterm exams). You don’t need to divulge your personal life circumstances, just let your team know if you anticipate being away at a certain time, and how you’ll stay on top of your work.
WarningFAQs
  • How often do we actually need to meet?

3. Conflict resolution strategies

  1. What should the procedure be for students that consistently display a pattern of not completing work?
  2. How will your group use AI in the project? Some people prefer never to use it, others rely heavily on it. Come up with a compromise that everyone is comfortable with, if possible.

  • What should we do if a member isn’t contributing?

Part 2: Project Proposal

Use the following subsections to ensure your Proposal contains the required information. It is the QA/QC Manager’s responsibility to check your submission against these specifications and the rubric.

1. Domain and Project Motivation

What is the Big Idea for your Project? What is the domain, who are your target users, and what tasks/analysis questions should they be able to complete with your project?

Tip

To determine these questions, do a quick internet search like you did for your Pitch to see what’s currently being done. You’ll have a chance to refine these ideas further. A great place to discover un-solved tasks in a vis area is to check “Future Work” sections of peer-reviewed publications.

When describing your analysis questions/tasks, be sure to use the concepts from Chapters 2 & 3 (Data Abstraction, Task Abstraction) in the textbook to write good tasks that are appropriate to the data types and dataset types you have identified. At least one of your analysis questions/tasks should require deriving new data/transforming data.

2. Data

Identify one or more preliminary dataset(s) that would support the tasks you identify.

Important

Be sure to cite your dataset.

Dataset requirements

Your dataset should be rich enough to support multiple analysis tasks and meaningful visual encodings. Very simple two- or three- column tables typically only support a single chart type and do not provide enough structure for a semester-long project.

Datasets with only two attributes will not be approved unless you justify why the structure is inherently complex (e.g., network edges, text corpora, spatial geometries). You can also add auxiliary data (for example, adding spatial boundaries from a 2nd source to categorical place names in a simple 2-column dataset dataset).

Detail the types, semantics, and limitations

  1. For each attribute you plan to use, include a description of the data type(s) and the semantics of the attributes (putting this information in a table in your doc might be good for this).
NoteReminders

Attribute semantics are the real-world meaning of that attribute. A dataset can have multiple types; which you choose is a design choice.

  1. Additionally, mention some limitations of the dataset(s). A limitation is anything about the data that could affect the analysis or conclusions. Examples include:

Size and Access

  • Dataset too large to store
  • Can only be streamed via API

Coverage and Granularity

  • Only includes one country, platform, or time period
  • Missing certain populations or categories
  • Lacks geographic detail
  • Aggregated at too course resolution for the task

Missing or Noisy Values

  • Many nulls or nans
  • Inconsistent categories or labels

Provenance

  • Unknown data collection and processing methods
  • Insufficient or dubious documentation

If you choose from the above list, or find a different limitation, explain how this limitation might affect your user’s ability to complete the task(s).

Frequently Asked Questions

NoteCan we switch roles during the project?

Yes! These should be considered default roles, but that doesn’t mean that you can’t switch it up throughout the project if something isn’t working, or situations evolve; just make sure the whole team agrees and update your charter accordingly.

NoteHow many code monkeys do we need?

The number of code monkeys you may need will depend on what format you choose for your developed software. Most groups will need at least one, but complex interactive systems may benefit from having two or more people focused on coding.

NoteHow often do we actually need to meet?

You don’t need to meet every week at your designated time, but having that time blocked ensures you can meet when needed. The frequency will likely vary throughout the project - more meetings during intensive phases, fewer when everyone is working independently on their roles.

NoteWhat if group members disagree about AI usage?

This is exactly why it should be discussed in your charter! I recommend using the “stoplight chart” format that you find on my instructions. Find a middle ground that everyone is comfortable with. For example, you might agree that AI can be used for technical debugging and research, but not for generating core content. Document your group’s policy clearly so everyone knows the boundaries.

NoteWhat should we do if a member isn’t contributing?

Follow the conflict resolution strategies you outlined in your charter. Typically, this involves: (1) addressing the issue directly with the team member first, (2) documenting the situation, (3) notifying the instructor if the pattern continues. Your charter should specify these steps clearly so everyone knows the process.

NoteDo we have to use specific tools?

The tools listed are suggestions, except for Google Drive. Using the Discord channels I set up is highly recommended. Choose whatever tools work best for your team’s workflow. The important thing is that everyone agrees on what you’ll use and has access to those tools. Document your choices in your charter.

NoteWhat if we’re not sure how to abstract our tasks?

Review Chapters 2 & 3 in the textbook and the Abstraction Activity materials. Every task should be described as an {action, target} pair. If you’re stuck, try describing what your user needs to do (the action: locate, compare, summarize, etc.) and what they’re doing it to (the target: outliers, trends, distributions, etc.). You can also ask for help during office hours.

NoteWhat counts as having “access” to the data?

You must be able to actually download or stream the data right now. “We applied for API access” or “We’re waiting for approval” doesn’t count. You should be able to open the data file and verify it contains what you expect. If you need special permissions, factor in that wait time or choose a different dataset.

NoteCan we use multiple datasets?

Yes! Many projects benefit from combining multiple data sources. Just make sure you cite all datasets and describe how you’ll integrate them.

NoteDo we need to decide on our final format now?

You should have a general idea, but the specifics can evolve. The important thing is to start thinking about what tools and techniques might be needed. You’ll refine this based on feedback and as you prototype a bunch of options.

NoteWhat if we forsee no issues?

There’s always the possibility for issues. Dig deep!
